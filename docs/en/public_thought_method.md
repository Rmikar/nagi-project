# How to Cultivate Public Thought
## — Opening beautiful language to criticism and correction

Nagi is a public philosophy, not for imposing conclusions, but for thinking together about a freer and safer society.

For that reason, it does not conflate facts, interpretations, norms, proposals, metaphors, and unresolved questions. When more than one of these appears in a chapter, headings, notes, sources, and conditions should make clear what kind of claim is being made.

## Six layers to distinguish

- **Fact**: Events, materials, institutions, and data that can be checked. State sources and the relevant date.
- **Interpretation**: A way of reading what facts may mean. Do not hide that other interpretations may exist.
- **Norm**: A value judgment about what should be protected. Do not present it as though it were a universal fact.
- **Proposal**: A possible institution, technology, or experiment. State assumptions, interests, alternatives, and what could happen if it fails.
- **Metaphor**: Words such as Breathing, Resonance, and Compass that help people see the world differently. Do not confuse them with exact technical specifications.
- **Unresolved question**: A matter whose answer is not yet known. Do not rush to present it as settled.

## Questions before publishing

- Who is being overlooked?
- Does beautiful language make responsibility or conflicting interests vague?
- Are words such as culture or resonance being used to rename inequality, extraction, or unpaid labor?
- When something fails, who bears what burden?
- Does publication increase understanding and verification, or does it remove someone's safety or context?

When AI is used for drafting, summarizing, translation, or research support, do not hide sources, limits, or unverified points. Treat AI-generated material as material for review, not as verified evidence in itself.

Publicness is not the quantity of exposure. It is an increase in criticizability, corrigibility, and accountability.

Private experience, health, family, intimate relationships, and information about people who have not chosen publication must not become evidence or content for a philosophy.

> Nagi is not a completed answer.  
> It grows by finding weaknesses, correcting them, and learning through experiments in writing, institutions, technology, and organization.
