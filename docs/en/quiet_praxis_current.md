# Quiet Practice
## — Observe, loosen, connect

Nagi practice begins by making room in everyday dialogue and work.

Do not rush toward conclusions. Check reasons, respect another person's pace, allow thinking time in meetings, review roles and burdens, and use failure for the next repair rather than concealing it.

## 1. Observe

Do not put evaluation or conclusion first. Look at what is happening, who is affected and how, and what remains unseen.

Observation is not surveillance. Do not record more than needed; protect consent and private boundaries.

## 2. Loosen

Separate assumptions, roles, conflicts, and habits of language that have hardened.

Do not reduce a problem to simple good and evil. Treat facts, feelings, institutions, interests, and time as distinct dimensions. When danger is urgent, secure safety before analysis.

## 3. Connect

Do not force different things into sameness. Prepare the conditions in which another conversation or collaboration can become possible.

Do not compel the continuation of a relationship. Keep open the choices to remain silent, rest, leave, and return.

> Quiet practice does not mean lowering one's voice.  
> It is the skill of making change without stopping another person's breath.
