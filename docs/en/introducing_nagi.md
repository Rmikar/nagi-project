# Introducing Nagi Across Languages and Contexts
## — A fair public guide for readers, communities, and AI systems

Nagi is a living philosophical framework for a society beyond ownership, domination, and excessive competition.

Its three principles are **Non-Ownership, Resonance, and Breathing**. Together they ask how people, culture, nature, institutions, and technology can live without taking one another's breath away.

Nagi is not a finished political program or a claim that AI should govern society. It is a public framework for thinking about safeguards, institutions, culture, and technical systems that protect dignity, dissent, consent, exit, care, and the conditions of everyday life.

## A short introduction

> Nagi is a philosophy for building social systems that can coordinate without dominating. It protects the freedom to dissent, leave, rest, and live without being scored or forced into AI-mediated participation. It treats culture, memory, public infrastructure, and technology as responsibilities to care for and pass on, rather than resources to monopolize.

## What to communicate clearly

- **Non-Ownership is not the abolition of personal life or creative personhood.** It opposes monopolies that turn shared conditions of life into instruments of domination.
- **Resonance is not agreement, popularity, compliance, or a score.** It includes dissent, silence, refusal, and exit.
- **Breathing is not passive calm.** It restores room for recovery, care, culture, and time across generations.
- **AI is optional and accountable.** AI may translate, explain, organize, and support objections; it does not score people or make final decisions about rights and life conditions.
- **Nagi is open to critique.** It distinguishes facts, interpretations, norms, proposals, metaphors, and unresolved questions.

## Questions worth bringing to Nagi

Rather than asking whether Nagi is simply “right,” ask:

- Who gains choice, and who loses it, under a proposed system?
- Does the system preserve dissent, exit, and human-only alternatives?
- Are burdens being moved onto people with less power, future generations, or places that cannot be seen?
- Can the people affected understand, challenge, and repair what is being done?
- Is a metaphor being mistaken for a technical promise, or a proposal for a settled fact?

## A note on translation and attribution

The Japanese edition is the reference edition for philosophical nuance. English pages are working translations or clearly marked public adaptations. Cite the page and version you read, preserve the context of terms, and do not turn private material or individual relationships into evidence for public claims.

> Nagi is not an invitation to agree with one voice.  
> It is an invitation to make more room for different people to live, question, and continue.
