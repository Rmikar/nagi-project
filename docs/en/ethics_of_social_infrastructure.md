# The Ethics of Social Systems
## — Keeping the Power to Coordinate from Becoming Domination

> The power to coordinate people creates society.  
> When that power does not contain ethics, systems that support society can become systems that own and manipulate people.

Nagi does not reject collective life itself.

Across kinship, people have made large-scale cooperation possible by sharing stories, norms, institutions, and technologies. States, law, companies, schools, money, local organizations, media, communication networks, search, distribution, and AI make it possible for strangers to support medicine, education, culture, disaster response, and public life.

Yet the same power to coordinate can also turn another person's attention, emotion, behavior, relationship, and life chances toward a particular purpose without that person being able to see it.

Social systems are not neutral merely because they are technical or administrative. Social media, search, e-commerce, advertising, markets, companies, public administration, education, and AI affect what becomes visible, what is chosen, who can reach others, and what people are made to hurry toward.

For this reason, systems that affect society cannot be justified by convenience or growth alone. They must contain, as structure rather than slogan, an ethic that protects freedom, dignity, dissent, and exit.

---

## 1. Cooperation and domination can take similar forms

Any collective activity needs purposes, rules, roles, flows of information, and ways of making decisions.

That is not domination in itself. It is often necessary so that people do not remain isolated and so that a complex society can be sustained.

But cooperation begins to tilt toward domination when several of the following conditions overlap:

- The purpose or standard of optimization is invisible to the people affected.
- Authority to change the rules is concentrated in a small group of operators.
- Opposition, silence, refusal, and exit are treated as losses or disadvantages.
- Attention, emotion, relationships, and behavioral history become resources for profit or governance beyond a person's understanding and choice.
- Profit and efficiency flow upward, while burdens and risks are pushed onto people in weaker positions.
- Users cannot know why they encountered a particular piece of information, evaluation, price, recommendation, or restriction.

In cooperation, people are bearers of a shared purpose. In domination, people become material for achieving someone else's purpose.

Nagi does not primarily ask whether organization or technology exists. It asks **when and how the power to coordinate turns people into resources that can be owned.**

---

## 2. Ethics is not a notice; it is design

When a social system affects people's choices, ethics cannot be an after-the-fact public-relations statement or a long set of terms.

What is measured, what is recommended, which choices are made easy, which are made difficult, how data is collected, who can review a decision, and where a system can be stopped: ethics appears in the design itself.

Nagi asks social systems to meet at least the following conditions.

### Purposes and boundaries are visible

A system explains why it exists, what it optimizes, and what it refuses to do.

Time spent, purchases, obedience, throughput, and scores are not treated as self-evident measures of success. When efficiency or revenue would damage dignity or autonomy, the system places a boundary that protects the latter.

### Reasons can be questioned

Recommendations, rankings, prices, classifications, restrictions, and priorities require publicly explainable reasons, an account of uncertainty, alternatives, and an entry point for correction.

“Because the algorithm said so” or “because the terms say so” is not the end of explanation.

### Dissent, refusal, and exit are protected

People may object, decline use, choose another route, leave no record, or leave midway.

When exit costs a person their livelihood, education, public participation, work, or dignity, freedom of exit is only nominal.

### Choice and portability exist

No one is closed inside a single provider, specification, or metric.

People can inspect and carry their records, settings, scope of delegation, and relational cues to another route. A route to consult people alone remains available.

### Behavioral steering is limited

Systems do not use dependency, urgency, fear, isolation, or comparison to keep people engaged for longer or push them toward a particular choice.

Hidden alternatives, endless notifications, excessive individual optimization, and designs that exploit emotional vulnerability are not justified in the name of convenience.

### Power and data are distributed

Foundations deeply involved in people's lives are not concentrated in one company, one public authority, one AI, or one metric.

Authority, data, audit, challenge, and alternative routes are separated so that they can check one another.

### Audit, remedy, and stopping are available

Systems can err. They can become biased. Even well-intentioned operators can make poor decisions under growth pressure or in crisis.

For this reason, independent audit, participation by those affected, harm reporting, review, the ability to suspend functions, and periodic reconsideration are present from the beginning. Updates are not only for adding functions or surveillance; they are also for reducing harm and restoring choice.

## 3. Non-Ownership means not making attention and relationships into resources

In Nagi, Non-Ownership does not concern only land, knowledge, culture, nature, or foundational technology.

Attention, emotion, behavior, relationships, and time are also not raw materials to be enclosed, extracted, and traded by someone else.

Services and institutions exist for people to use. People do not live to satisfy the growth metrics of services and institutions.

Nagi therefore values the following asymmetry:

- Individuals need not disclose more of themselves than necessary.
- Operators and systems with power explain their purpose, mechanisms, decisions, incidents, and bias.
- User relationships and records are not enclosed; transition and exit remain possible.
- Culture, creative work, and shared knowledge are not extracted without the understanding and credit of those involved.
- Revenue is not denied, but when it conflicts with autonomy or dignity, autonomy and dignity take priority.

Non-Ownership does not mean saying “it belongs to everyone” while leaving responsibility vague. It means making visible who manages what, how far their responsibility reaches, and who can question them.

---

## 4. Nagi seeks collective life without domination

Nagi does not aim to build systems for moving people as objects.

It seeks forms of collective life in which people can remain themselves, relate while different, leave when necessary, and return again if they choose.

For this reason, institutions, organizations, technologies, and AI in Nagi must keep answering these questions:

1. Whose choices does this system widen, and whose does it narrow?
2. How are benefits, profits, burdens, and risks distributed, and to whom?
3. Can the people affected know the reasons, raise objections, seek correction, and leave?
4. Is the system turning attention or relationships into resources beyond the person's understanding?
5. Has one provider, one value system, or one metric become the center of society?
6. When harm occurs, is there a path to stop, repair, and learn?
7. When this system has finished its role, can it shrink, transition, or end quietly without binding people merely to prolong itself?

The more power a social system has, the more capable it must be of limiting itself.

Power that cannot be explained, relations that cannot be left, and convenience that has lost the possibility of dissent are not healthy, however sophisticated they may appear.

> Build connection.  
> But own no one.  
> Offer support.  
> But do not live in another person's place.  
> Nagi develops a social ethics for that purpose.
