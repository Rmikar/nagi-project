# Cultural Co-Growth
## — Passing culture among people who live differently, without harming them

Culture is not an ornament for leisure.

It is the work through which people cultivate their place, memories, language, and relationships, and pass them to the next generation.

Nagi does not reduce culture to products or popularity alone. Those who create, receive, sustain within a community, and quietly witness all participate in cultural circulation in different ways.

## 1. The personhood and context of creators

A work carries a creator's name, relationships, land, era, and language.

Use and sharing do not mean erasing this context so that the work can be used freely. The creator's name and intention are respected, and alteration, reuse, and publication are handled with care.

## 2. Opening and protecting

Opening culture matters. But not everything should be made public without condition.

Local knowledge, vulnerable memories, and expressions connected to people directly affected require an appropriate scope of publication, consent, the ability to stop, and contextual explanation.

When openness becomes extraction, choosing not to open is also a way of protecting culture.

## 3. AI and culture

AI can support translation, preservation, accessibility, and the organization of materials.

But learning from, reusing, or commercializing works and memories without consent is not cultural circulation.

AI should not level the voices of creators and communities into one average expression. It should work so that differences can remain.

## 4. Silence and the freedom not to receive

Culture also includes time not spent creating, time not spent speaking, and the choice not to receive.

Participation and expression are not conditions of contribution to culture. A place remains for people who keep a quiet distance.

## 5. The responsibility of those who inherit

To inherit culture is not to freeze the past or turn the lives of creators and communities into material for public exposure.

Record provenance, contribution, citation, and reasons for decisions. Respect not only visible achievement, but also less visible work such as maintenance, translation, care, and record-keeping.

When an error or harm occurs, do not end with concealment or condemnation alone. Recognize the impact, make necessary corrections and explanations, and examine the systems that allowed harm to recur.

> Culture does not exist to be owned by someone.  
> It exists to pass between people who live differently, without harming them.
