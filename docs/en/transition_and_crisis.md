# Transition and Crisis Design
## — To stay with reality and protect people when things break

Nagi does not speak only of a completed distant future. The societies where people already live contain employment, families, debt, illness, housing, regional inequality, war, disasters, discrimination, and violence.

A rush toward the future must not leave behind people who are already in vulnerable positions. Transition into Nagi is not a revolution that destroys existing safeguards. It is a process of strengthening what protects life while gradually loosening monopoly and domination.

---

## 1. First principle of transition: do not retreat from protections that already exist

Nagi does not begin by replacing existing social security, labor protections, human rights, welfare, or public services.

- Basic Breath must not become less stable than existing safeguards.
- Participation in an experiment or use of AI must never be a condition of receiving support.
- Changes to systems affecting daily life require small-scale trials, public evaluation, and the possibility of reversal.
- Even when an existing system is flawed, do not overlook the people whom it is currently protecting.

> A new system must not make people feel less secure before it can protect them better.  
> This is Nagi's minimum condition for transition.

---

## 2. Four stages of transition

### Stage 1: Try things close to life

Practice Non-Ownership, Resonance, and Breathing in small ways in schools, workplaces, neighborhoods, and creative communities.

Examples include reusable educational materials, clear credit for collaborative work, meetings designed around rest, local cultural archives, and consultation routes that do not preserve conversation records.

The question at this stage is not whether an initiative looks successful. It is whether people who did not participate were kept safe from harm.

### Stage 2: Increase institutional choices

Create more Nagi-like choices inside existing businesses, local governments, schools, and cultural institutions.

- Rules for sharing and reuse.
- Transparent decision records.
- Consent-based data management.
- Small funds returned to culture, education, and care.
- Human-only routes for consultation and challenge.

Nagi exists not to impose one method on everyone, but to increase choices beyond monopoly and isolation.

### Stage 3: Cultivate publicness

Develop foundational AI, educational resources, local memory, environmental data, and cultural resources as public trusts rather than the property of a few.

This requires operational transparency, independent audit, local autonomy, participation by those affected, and real possibilities for exit.

### Stage 4: A federated commons

Different regions, cultures, and institutions do not submit to one center. They learn from one another and connect only the parts that need to connect.

Nagi's destination is not one world system. It is a **federated commons** in which diverse societies can help one another without being dominated.

---

## 3. Principles in crisis

Disaster, epidemic, violence, evacuation, and resource scarcity are moments when ordinary agreement alone may not be enough. Nagi does not allow crisis to become a reason to take freedom and dignity away permanently.

### Prioritize life and safety

In crisis, life, bodily safety, evacuation, medical care, food, water, and communication come first. This priority does not change according to reputation, resonance, economic status, nationality, gender, disability, beliefs, or AI use.

### Allocate scarce resources through visible criteria

When medical care, electricity, housing, or food are insufficient, the criteria must be publicly explainable: necessity, urgency, degree of harm, and the presence or absence of alternatives.

- AI may help organize information and find bias.
- AI does not become the final allocator.
- Exceptional decisions include records, time limits, and independent review.

### Emergency powers must end

Special authority, data use, movement restrictions, and priority allocation during emergencies must have a stated reason for starting, an end date, conditions for release, and a way to seek review.

Surveillance or exclusion introduced in crisis must not become the ordinary system afterward.

---

## 4. Violence and discrimination

Nagi values dialogue and resonance. But violence, threats, exploitation, and discrimination cannot always be resolved through dialogue alone.

- Protect the safety and choices of the person harmed first.
- Do not determine harm or danger solely from community mood or AI inference.
- Provide independent routes for consultation, protection, investigation, and remedy.
- Restorative dialogue is appropriate only when the harmed person wants it and safety is secure.
- Build conditions of participation and challenge so that people vulnerable to discrimination do not disappear as minority voices.

Resonance is never a reason to look away from violence.

---

## 5. Responsibility across borders

Nagi's technology, resources, culture, and AI do not arise only from the surplus of wealthy regions. Raw materials, labor, environmental burdens, language resources, and histories of extraction are often involved invisibly.

Nagi therefore continues to ask:

- Who receives the benefits and who carries the burdens?
- Are local self-determination and cultural consent protected?
- Are data and knowledge being extracted from outside?
- Are environmental costs being pushed into places that cannot be seen?

“Sharing” must not become a word through which the powerful take from the less powerful.

---

## 6. Record failure and make return possible

Nagi's experiments do not collect only success.

Within the limits of what can be publicly shared, they record:

- What did not work.
- Who found it difficult to participate.
- Which data proved unnecessary.
- Which decisions lacked explanation.
- Which systems were stopped, rolled back, or remade.

> Nagi does not rush toward certainty.  
> It seeks a society that can return, learn again, and protect again when something breaks.
