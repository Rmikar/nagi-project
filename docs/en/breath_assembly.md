# 🫧 Breath Assembly Protocol
## — Holding together decision-making and life across difference

A Breath Assembly is not a meeting designed to lead everyone to the same conclusion.

It is a protocol for carefully distinguishing what should be decided together from what should remain with individuals and communities, while preserving different positions, tempos, silence, and opposition.

In Nagi, Resonance is not agreement of opinion. It is finding the cooperation that is necessary after recognizing difference without stopping another person's breath.

---

## 1. Preconditions of a Breath Assembly

### Participation is voluntary

No one is compelled to participate in a meeting, use AI support, be recorded, or be analyzed.

People who cannot participate, choose not to speak, or leave halfway retain basic rights and access to life.

### Silence is not consent

Silence may mean reflection, fatigue, fear, a language barrier, or disagreement.

It is not automatically recorded as approval or “quiet resonance.”

### Some problems cannot be resolved through dialogue alone

Violence, discrimination, rights violations, and urgent safety cannot be left to dialogue alone.

Protection, independent consultation, clear procedures, and—when needed—public responsibility remain in place.

---

## 2. A polycentric field, not a hierarchy

A Breath Assembly is not a hierarchy flowing from top to bottom.

Multiple settings sit alongside one another: local communities, themes, directly affected people, professions, and generations. Each has its own proper sphere of judgment.

| Setting | Primary role | What it protects |
|---|---|---|
| Small dialogue circles | Consultation close to daily life, shared practice, mutual understanding | no forced exposure |
| Thematic assemblies | Design and coordination across different positions | both lived experience and expertise |
| Public forums | Broad resources, institutions, and policy | public reasons, dissent, records, and review |
| Independent remedy routes | Objections, harms, and procedural failures | independence from operators, AI, and majorities |

A decision in one setting does not dominate all others. Matters that closer participants can decide remain there whenever possible.

---

## 3. Different kinds of decisions

Not everything is decided through the same meeting format.

### A. Everyday cooperation

Events, collaborative work, and small local adjustments proceed through dialogue, agreement, experimentation, and short reviews.

### B. Allocation of scarce or public resources

For facilities, grants, time, space, or environmental resources, decisions combine necessity, impact, alternatives, voices of people affected, public reasons, and the possibility of challenge.

Metrics are supporting material, never an automatic selection device.

### C. Decisions about rights, safety, and sanctions

For basic life protection, discrimination, violence, personal data, or suspension of access, neither the quantity of Resonance nor majority opinion decides the matter.

These require clear rights standards, independent review, remedy, time limits, and reconsideration.

---

## 4. Basic process

1. **Make the question public**: clarify what will and will not be decided, and who will be affected.
2. **Offer information in more than one form**: place lived narratives, expertise, local memory, costs, environmental impacts, and counter-hypotheses alongside one another.
3. **Receive objections first**: before counting approval, ask what is dangerous and who might be left behind.
4. **Make time for reflection**: do not rush a conclusion in the room; provide asynchronous input or smaller dialogue when needed.
5. **Revise the proposal**: do not “process” opposition; show how it changed the proposal.
6. **Decide with reasons and limits**: attach reasons, dissenting views, time limits, review points, and routes for remedy.
7. **Re-breathe after implementation**: examine whom the decision actually helped and burdened; stop and repair it when necessary.

---

## 5. Dissonance protocol

Failure to reach agreement is not failure.

When dissonance arises, make the available paths visible:

- revise and propose again;
- try on a small scale;
- implement several options in parallel;
- defer the decision;
- return judgment to people closer to the matter;
- send it to independent mediation or review; or
- separate the parts that do not require a shared decision.

Do not say “it became quiet, so it passed.” Confirm that reasons for opposition became visible and were addressed.

---

## 6. The role of AI

AI does not become the center of an assembly or its judge. Whether to use AI is chosen by participants and people affected.

AI may:

- summarize and translate materials accessibly;
- organize issues, people affected, and counter-hypotheses;
- find minority views and unanswered questions;
- record decision reasons and review dates; and
- support accessibility.

AI must not:

- score a person's emotions or character;
- turn silence into approval;
- automatically adopt a proposal;
- decide rights, basic life protection, or sanctions; or
- treat dissent and exit as lack of cooperation.

AI-use records, data used, and limitations are disclosed in understandable form.

---

## 7. In emergencies

Even in an emergency, do not normalize a structure in which AI or a small number of operators decides alone.

- Prioritize life and bodily safety.
- Give exceptional measures an end date and conditions for release.
- Make it possible to examine the reasons for emergency judgment afterward.
- Preserve routes for consultation, review, and remedy for people affected.

See [Transition and Crisis Design](transition_and_crisis.html) for further detail.

---

## Closing

> A Breath Assembly is not an apparatus for making the world into one voice.  
> It is a place where different voices can decide what is necessary together without taking one another's breath away.
