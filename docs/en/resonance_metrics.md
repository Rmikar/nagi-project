# 🌐 Resonance Metrics
## — Finding institutional suffocation without scoring people

In contemporary society, views, sales, stock prices, standardized scores, efficiency, and ratings often come to stand in for the value of people and places.

Nagi values culture, care, rest, and local relationships that cannot be seen through numbers alone.

But merely giving numbers another name repeats the same domination. If the word “Resonance” becomes a score for human worth, obedience, or social fitness, Nagi would reproduce the ownership and domination it seeks to loosen.

For that reason, Nagi's Resonance Metrics **do not score people.**

What they examine is not a person, but the breathing of institutions and shared settings.

---

## 1. Resonance is not consent or popularity

Resonance does not mean that everyone shares one view.

It is what happens when encountering someone's words, work, action, or silence changes how another person sees the world. Relations form without erasing difference; the world becomes more deeply visible through difference, including opposition and distance when they are necessary.

Resonance is therefore not the same as:

- popularity;
- follower counts;
- amount of speech;
- number of times a person participates;
- behavior that is easy for AI to read;
- appearing quiet and cooperative; or
- agreeing with the majority of a community.

Silence, refusal, short involvement, anger, and exit are not excluded from Nagi.

---

## 2. Change what is measured

Nagi does not seek to answer, “Who is a valuable person?”

> **Does this institution, this community, this public service create conditions in which people can breathe without undue strain?**

Metrics do not rank individual character or contribution. They return questions to the institution.

| Perspective | Question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Basic Breath | Can people who do not participate still access life? | Are support and access separated from evaluation and AI use? |
| Possibility of exit | Can someone stop midway without disadvantage? | Are deletion, withdrawal, and another route available? |
| Possibility of challenge | Are dissenting views addressed? | Are objections recorded, reviewed, and answered? |
| Transparency | Can people understand the reasons for decisions? | Are allocation reasons, data used, and responsible roles public? |
| Care and fairness | Is burden concentrated on particular people? | Are rest, accessibility, and correction of discrimination present? |
| Freedom from surveillance | Is observation truly minimal? | Can emotion inference be stopped? Are retention periods and anonymization defined? |
| Continuity | Are culture, nature, and relationships being depleted? | Is there local return, attention to environmental impact, and room for inheritance? |

Nagi calls this set of questions the **Institutional Breath Index (IBI).**

---

## 3. IBI is diagnosis, not judgment

IBI is a bundle of questions for examining an institution's condition. It is not compressed into a single total score. It does not decide, by number alone, which settings are superior or inferior.

- Read numbers alongside stories, voices of people affected, minority views, and observation in the field.
- Even when numbers rise, review the system if someone is suffering.
- Even when numbers are low, do not punish a setting that is supporting people in difficult conditions.
- Do not force values that escape the metric to obey the metric.

Metrics are a compass, not a report card.

---

## 4. Handling data

Resonance Metrics are not made through constant surveillance.

### Principles

1. **Participation is voluntary**: declining does not remove Basic Breath or social standing.
2. **Respect self-report**: a person can choose not to speak.
3. **Prefer aggregation**: examine institutional tendencies in ways that do not identify individuals.
4. **Do not make emotion inference the default**: do not infer emotion from voice, facial expression, writing style, or behavior without specific consent and purpose.
5. **Do not retain too much**: delete or anonymize data once its purpose is complete.
6. **Build in challenge**: people can challenge records, classifications, and aggregation.

See [Memory, Consent, and the Right to Forget](memory_and_consent.html) for further detail.

---

## 5. Distance from resource allocation

Nagi questions a structure in which facilities, time, care, and cultural resources are decided only by concentrated wealth. But IBI never decides allocation automatically.

Decisions involving resources combine at least:

- basic need;
- voices of people and communities concerned;
- public reasons;
- objections and alternatives;
- effects on minority positions;
- long-term ecological and cultural effects; and
- independent review and reconsideration.

AI and metrics may help find bias or shortage. They do not make final decisions about basic life protections, rights restrictions, sanctions, or life-saving allocation.

---

## 6. The role of AI: a stethoscope for dissent, not a grader

AI can make institutional explanations easier to understand and help find overlooked voices and bias.

AI may:

- organize decision reasons and data used;
- present multiple interpretations, counter-hypotheses, and minority views;
- reduce accessibility and language barriers; and
- make changes over time and regional disparities visible.

AI must not:

- fix people as “high resonance” or “low resonance”;
- infer emotion, character, or political stance for allocation;
- treat dissent or silence as lack of cooperation;
- hide reasons for judgment; or
- bypass human review, objection, and remedy.

---

## 7. What experiments should test

In experiments such as the Nagi Lab, the question is not “Was Resonance high?” The questions are:

- Could people who declined AI analysis participate safely?
- Did people who raised objections avoid disadvantage?
- Did people who exited keep their livelihood and relationships?
- Were burdens avoided for people who did not participate?
- Could collected data be reduced?
- Did explanation and routes for challenge actually work?

> People do not adapt themselves to a metric.  
> The metric must continually be asked whether it protects people's freedom.
