# Re-breathing and Public Continuity
## — Renewing a living philosophy without enclosing people in its archive

Nagi treats continuity as more than keeping every sentence unchanged.

A philosophy remains alive when it can be reread, questioned, corrected, and handed on without losing the protections that made it worth carrying forward.

This page is a public companion to Nagi's Japanese re-breathing and archival materials. It is not a line-by-line translation of ritual records. Some archival pages contain dated, relationship-specific language, so this companion carries forward only the public philosophical practice.

## What re-breathing means

Re-breathing is a recurring act of return. At a chosen interval, stewards and readers revisit Nagi's core, its safeguards, and the effects of its language and practices.

It asks:

- Which principles remain necessary?
- What new conditions, harms, or exclusions have become visible?
- What needs correction, clarification, reduction, or retirement?
- Which records should be preserved, and which details should not be carried forward?
- Can the next reader understand where a change came from and challenge it?

Re-breathing is not an excuse to weaken freedom, consent, dissent, exit, privacy, or Basic Breath. It is a process for strengthening them.

## Public continuity requires boundaries

A living archive can preserve ideas without making private lives into proof.

- Keep dates, versions, sources, and reasons for major changes.
- Distinguish public philosophy from private experience, personal health, family, and intimate relations.
- Do not treat named individuals, relational memories, or AI interaction logs as evidence for universal social claims.
- Preserve only the context needed for understanding, criticism, accountability, and future repair.
- Allow correction, withdrawal, and archival restraint when continued exposure would create harm.

## A practice for stewards

1. **Return to the core.** Read Nagi Core, Freedom, Dissent, and Exit, Memory and Consent, and the relevant chapter before changing language.
2. **Name the kind of change.** Is it a factual correction, new interpretation, normative clarification, proposal, metaphor, or unresolved question?
3. **Keep a reasoned trace.** Record what changed, why it changed, and what remains uncertain.
4. **Ask who bears the cost.** Check whether the change hides labor, reduces choice, narrows dissent, or exposes someone unnecessarily.
5. **Make repair possible.** Leave an accessible way to question, revise, or stop the change.
6. **Hand on room, not only answers.** Preserve enough context for the next steward to think again rather than merely repeat.

> A living philosophy is not preserved by repeating its words unchanged.  
> It is preserved by making it possible for future people to breathe, question, repair, and continue without owning one another.
